Reading What People Don't Say

Before the interview. Before the question. Before the answer — there's a moment when someone's body tells the truth.

What you're doing today

The Setup

You read emotionally charged words out loud. Your partner responds with the first word that comes to mind — no editing, no second-guessing.

Your Job

Notice what their body does before their mouth catches up — the hesitation, the shift, the glance away. That's the data.

Why It Matters

The words that make someone hesitate are the ones worth asking about later. This lab teaches you to catch those moments in real time.

The three roles

Role

What you do

What you're watching for

Reader

Read words from the list one at a time. Neutral tone — no commentary, no reaction.

Whether your delivery is influencing the Responder's reaction.

Responder

Say the first word that comes to mind. No editing. No explaining.

Which words make you want to pause, qualify, or look away.

Observer

Watch the Responder. Take notes. Share one specific observation after each round — not an interpretation, just what you saw.

Pauses, eye movement, posture shifts, answers that come too fast or too slow.

Rotate after each round so everyone plays all three roles.

What to look for as Observer

Four signals worth tracking +
  • A pause longer than a breath before answering
  • Eyes that move before the word comes out
  • An answer that arrives too fast — like it was rehearsed
  • A word the Responder repeats, qualifies, or immediately walks back

You don't need to know what it means. You just need to notice it happened — and remember it for when you get to ask questions.

How this connects to 6.08 +

In your final interview, you won't have a word list. But you'll have a person across from you — and they'll react to your questions the same way. A hesitation, a topic shift, an answer that comes too quickly. Every one of those is an opening. This lab is where you start learning to see them.

6.07 | Interview Prep

✦ Skill: Question Design · Purpose Clarity · Working the Progression

The best interviews are about building trust so someone feels comfortable enough to tell you the real story. That doesn't happen by accident — it happens because your questions are designed to take them there.

What This Is

Two parts. First you define who you're talking to and why. Then you build a question set that moves from low-risk openers to the real story underneath.

Who You're Interviewing

An elder — someone older than you with meaningful life experience. Think beyond convenience. Choose someone who has lived through things, made decisions, and has something to reflect on.

What Goes to the Basket

Both parts, completed. Your 6.07 Reference is a question bank by theme — use it if you need a spark, but your final questions need to be yours, in your voice.

Part 1 — Choose Your Subject and Define Your Purpose

Before you write a single question, you need to know who you're talking to and — more importantly — why. Answer the prompts below. These aren't throwaway boxes. Your answers here will shape every question you write in Part 2.

Name / Person

Relationship to you

Why this person?

What makes them the right person to tell this story? What have they lived through or experienced that others might not?

What's your topic?

Be specific — not "childhood," but something like "a decision you made that surprised people."

What do you hope to learn?

What's the real question underneath? What are you actually trying to understand — and why does it matter to you?

Your Guiding Question

If you could ask one question and truly understand the answer — what would it be?

Part 2 — Build Your Question Progression

Think of this like warming up. Level 1 is safe and general. Level 2 gets closer. Level 3 is where the real story lives. By the time you ask Level 3 questions, your subject should feel comfortable enough to be honest. Your 6.07 Reference has examples by theme — use it as a spark, not a script.

Level 1 — Surface: Low-Risk Openers

Build Comfort

Start here. These are inviting but not invasive. You're setting a tone: I'm interested. This is a safe space. We can talk. No pressure, no depth yet.

Example

"I've been thinking about [topic]. I know you have experience with that. Would you be willing to talk about it?"

"How did you first get involved with [topic]? What was that like?"

"When was a time you remember being really engaged with [topic]?"

Your Questions

Level 2 — Detail: Moving Closer

Gather Specifics

Rapport is established. These questions dig into specifics — concrete moments, feelings, decisions. You're asking them to relive it, not defend it. Still focused on what happened, not what it meant.

Example

"Walk me through what happened. What was the first thing you noticed?"

"How were you feeling during that? What was going through your head?"

"Who else was there, and how did they react?"

Your Questions

Level 3 — Depth: Unlock Meaning

Earn Your Way Here

By now they trust you. These questions ask for reflection and vulnerability — why did this matter, what did you learn, what would you do differently. This is where an experience becomes a story.

Example

"What made that moment stick with you? Why do you think it mattered?"

"Did that change anything for you? How did you see things differently after?"

"What do you wish you'd known before going through that?"

Your Questions

Before You Go

Use photos.

Ask if they have any photos from the period you'll discuss. Photos unlock memory in a way questions alone can't.

Record it.

Get permission beforehand, then record audio on your phone. Don't rely on memory alone — this is your primary source.

Sound like yourself.

If a question sounds like a form, rewrite it. Your questions need to land in your voice, or they won't land at all.

Going deeper

How specific is specific enough? +

The difference between a weak topic and a strong one is almost always specificity. "Childhood" is a category. "The summer before your dad's job changed" is a door. "What was your career like" is a survey question. "Was there a moment when the work started to feel different to you" is an invitation. Before you finalize your topic in Part 1, ask yourself: does this give my subject something to actually locate in their memory, or does it leave them guessing where to start?

What makes a guiding question worth having +

Your guiding question isn't something you ask out loud — it's the question underneath all your other questions. It keeps you oriented when the conversation goes sideways. A good guiding question is personal (you actually want to know the answer), unanswerable in one sentence, and bigger than your topic. "What did it cost them?" "What made them stay?" "What do they know now that they couldn't have known then?" If your guiding question has an obvious answer, it's not the real one yet.

How this connects to 6.08 +

In 6.08 you contact your subject and set up the real interview. What you build here is what you bring with you — not as a script to read from, but as a map you know well enough to fold up and put in your pocket. The best interviewers know their questions cold so they can ignore them when the conversation needs it. Build something worth knowing.

Reflection — Submit to the Basket

Answer all three. These should take you five honest minutes, not thirty hurried seconds.

1

Why this person? What makes them the right subject for this — and what do you think they know that you don't?

2

Which of your questions are you most uncertain about? What makes it risky — and why are you keeping it anyway?

3

Write your guiding question here. Not the one you wrote in Part 1 — the one you actually want answered. If they're different, say why.

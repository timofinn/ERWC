Narrative Medicine · Unit 6

6.05 | Listening With Purpose

✦ Skill: Emotional Tone · Responsive Questioning · Personal Connection

Three rounds. Three skills. Each one isolates something you should be doing in every real interview — so you can feel what it actually takes to do it well.

The Structure

Three rounds, each building on the last. You switch roles after each round so you practice both sides — as the speaker and as the listener.

The Setup

Sit facing your partner. Decide who starts as Speaker and who starts as Listener. You'll switch after each round — both people get every role.

By the End

You'll have practiced the three moves that separate a real interview from a list of questions read aloud.

The three rounds

Round 1 — Emotional Tone

3–4 min each

Speaker

Tell your partner about something that happened recently — anything. Keep talking for the full time, even if it feels like you've run out of things to say. Notice how your partner is receiving what you're saying.

Listener

Don't ask any questions. Just listen. If your partner finishes early, sit in silence until the time is up — don't fill it. When time is up: tell them what emotional shifts you noticed and exactly where. Not "you seemed sad" — but "your voice got quieter when you mentioned your brother."

Track it: What emotion did you pick up, and what specific moment triggered it? What did you do — or not do — in response?

Switch roles. Repeat Round 1 before moving on.

Round 2 — Responsive Questioning

3–4 min each

Speaker

New topic — something you haven't talked about yet. Keep going even when your listener's questions surprise you or take you somewhere unexpected.

Listener

You can ask questions now — but every question must grow directly from what your partner just said. No pivoting, no questions prepared in advance. Use paraphrasis first: "So what I'm hearing is... is that right?" Then follow up: "What do you mean by that?" / "Say more." / "What happened after?" One question per pause.

Track it: Which question felt most responsive? Which felt forced? Write one paraphrase you used — or wish you had.

Switch roles. Repeat Round 2 before moving on.

Round 3 — Personal Connection

4–5 min each

Speaker

New topic again. Talk about something with some personal weight — a decision you made, something that surprised you, a moment that changed how you thought about something.

Listener

Same rules as Round 2 — but now add one move: draw a brief personal connection before your next question. One sentence from your own experience that resonates. Then ask. Example: "That reminds me of a time I had to make a call without knowing if it was right — when you made that decision, did you feel like you had enough information?" The connection isn't about you. It's a bridge. Use it, then get out of the way.

Track it: Write the personal connection you made. Did it open your partner up or pull focus away? What would you do differently?

Switch roles. Repeat Round 3.

Going deeper

Why silence is an instruction +

In Round 1 the listener's only job is to sit with what the speaker is saying — no questions, no filler, no rescue. That discomfort you feel when silence stretches past what's normal? Your speaker feels it too, and most of the time they'll fill it with something more honest than what they would have said if you'd jumped in. Silence isn't passive. It's a move.

How personal connection can go wrong +

Personal connection works when it's a bridge — one sentence that signals "I recognize what you're describing," then a question that takes it further. It breaks down when it becomes a story about you. If your partner is talking less after you made the connection, you went too far. The test: did the focus shift back to them, or did it stay on you?

How this connects to 6.08 +

In your final interview you'll be deploying all three of these moves at once — reading emotional tone, responding to what was actually said, and bridging with personal connection when it earns the next question. Today you practice them one at a time so you can feel each one distinctly. By 6.08, they need to be automatic.

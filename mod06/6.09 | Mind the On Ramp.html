6.09 | Mind the On Ramp

✦ Skill: Validate · Contextualize · Ask

Rolling off a list of questions isn't an interview — it's a deposition. The art lies in how you connect them. Today you'll feel that difference firsthand.

Two parts: ask questions cold to feel what doesn't work, then build the three-move sequence and ask the same question the right way.

The Setup

Work with a partner. One person is the Interviewer, one is the Subject. You'll switch roles between Parts 1 and 2 — both people need to feel both sides of this.

The Three Moves

Validate — show you heard them. Contextualize — explain where the next question is coming from. Ask — then ask it. Same question, different approach. Pay attention to what changes.

The Point

You're using the same questions both times. The only variable is the on-ramp. Notice what changes in how your subject responds when you build it vs. when you skip it.

Part 1 — The Deposition

Cold Questions Only — No Setup, No Acknowledgment

Write 3 questions you'd want to ask your interview subject. Then sit with your partner — as soon as they finish responding, move straight to the next question. No paraphrasing, no acknowledgment, no on-ramp. Get an answer, ask the next one. Take quick notes on what you notice.

Going deeper

What validation actually sounds like +

Validation is not "wow, that's so interesting" or "I totally get that." Those are fillers — they don't show you were listening. Real validation reflects back something specific your subject actually said. If they said "I didn't feel like I had a choice," you might say: "So it wasn't a decision so much as a realization — is that right?" That proves you heard the thing underneath, not just the words.

When in doubt: paraphrase their specific language back to them, then check your understanding before moving on.

Why contextualizing feels awkward at first +

Most people skip contextualization because it feels like you're slowing the conversation down. You're not — you're building the road your question is about to travel on. Without it, your question can land sideways, even if it's a good one. Think of contextualization as the sentence that earns the question the right to be asked. One sentence: "I'm curious about X because Y." That's it. Then ask.

How this connects to 6.10 +

In 6.10 you bring everything together — your questions, your listening skills, and now the three-move sequence — into your actual interview. The on-ramp is the difference between an interview that feels like a conversation and one that feels like a form being filled out. By the time you sit down with your subject, this should be automatic.

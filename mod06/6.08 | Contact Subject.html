The Medium

Text, email, DM — whatever actually reaches this person. The format matters less than whether they'll see it. Know your subject well enough to know how to get to them.

Example message

This is a real example — adapt the tone to fit your relationship with your subject. Formal if needed, casual if that's right. The elements matter more than the wording.

Example — adapt to your own voice and relationship

Hey Grandpa,

I hope you're well. I'm reaching out because my English class is doing a project that requires each of us to interview someone who might have stories to share or wisdom to offer. You were the first person that sprang to mind.

I was hoping to get 15 minutes of your time to sit down and record a conversation in the next 3–5 days. I wanted to ask about the progression of your life — how you see the world differently now than you did 10, 20, or 40 years ago. Maybe some stories from when you were my age, and lessons you've picked up along the way about health, relationships, finances — whatever feels worth passing on.

One thing to know: I'll be recording the audio of our conversation as part of the assignment, so just a heads up on that. Also, if you happen to have any old photos that connect to stories you might want to tell, feel free to have those handy. I can send you a list of questions ahead of time if that would help you feel prepared — just let me know.

Would you be free this weekend or Monday afternoon? We're writing up a paper on this next week, so the sooner the better.

Best,
Timmy

What's happening in this message

Para 1

Context + why this person. Personal, not generic.

Para 2

The ask (15 min, 3–5 days) + the topics, so they know what's coming.

Para 3

Recording notice + photos + questions offer. All three in one pass.

Para 4

The rendezvous (specific options) + urgency. Easy for them to say yes.

Going deeper

What if they say they're too busy? +

Fifteen minutes is short on purpose. If they say they're busy, push back gently: "It's really just 15 minutes — I can come to you, wherever is easiest." Most people who hesitate will say yes if you make it low-lift enough. If they genuinely can't do it in the window, let Mr. Finn know today — not the day before the paper is due.

Should you send questions ahead of time? +

Offer it, but don't push it. Some subjects will feel more comfortable knowing what's coming — and a prepared subject often gives better, more considered answers. Others will give more honest, in-the-moment responses if they're going in cold. Read your person. If they're nervous, send the questions. If they're easy-going, surprise them.

How this connects to 6.09 +

In 6.09 you'll prepare for the first two minutes of your actual interview — the hardest part. Everything you wrote in this message is setting up that moment: your subject already knows what to expect, the recording is cleared, and the mood is warm before you've even sat down. The outreach isn't just logistics. It's the start of the interview.

6.09 | Examples in Action

✦ Reference Only — Aim for Exemplary. Settle for Par. Catch yourself if you're doing Subpar.

All three examples below use the same interview scenario and the same question. The only difference is how well the interviewer sets it up. Use this as a benchmark when writing your own sequences in Part 2.

The Scenario

Rosa — a grandmother who left her home country in her late twenties with almost nothing — just said:

"We didn't really talk about it like it was a choice. You just knew. You looked at your kids and you thought — not here. Not like this. And then you started figuring out how."

Three levels of execution — same question, different approach

Subpar

Ignores response, jumps to next question
1

Validate

No validation. The interviewer says nothing and moves directly to the next question.

2

Contextualize

No context given. Rosa has no idea why this next question is coming.

3

Ask

"Okay, so — what was your job like when you first got here?"

What to notice: The question lands cold. There's no acknowledgment of what Rosa just shared — one of the most consequential decisions of her life — and no signal about where the conversation is going. It feels transactional. Like her answer was a stepping stone, not something worth sitting with.

Meh. It's Aight.

Does the moves, but mechanically
1

Validate

"Wow, that must've been tough."

2

Contextualize

"Let's move on to what made you decide to go."

3

Ask

"What was the hardest part about leaving?"

What to notice: There's an attempt at all three moves — that puts this above Subpar. But "wow, that must've been tough" doesn't reflect anything Rosa actually said. It's generic sympathy, not real validation. The contextualization tells her what's coming, but not why the interviewer cares. The sequence is there. The attentiveness isn't.

Exemplary

Specific, thoughtful, human
1

Validate

"You said it didn't feel like a choice — that it was more like a realization you arrived at internally."

2

Contextualize

"I'm curious about the shift from the idea of leaving — to actually doing it."

3

Ask

"Was there a specific moment — something you saw or felt — that made it real? That you were actually going to leave?"

What to notice: The validation reflects back Rosa's specific language — "not like a choice," the knowing, the figuring out — and names the emotional logic underneath without projecting onto her. The contextualization makes clear what the interviewer is genuinely curious about: the gap between knowing and doing. By the time the question arrives, Rosa knows this person has been listening. The question feels like an invitation, not an interrogation.

What each move is actually doing

Move 1

Validate

Show them you actually heard what they said. Paraphrase their specific words back — not just the emotion, the idea. Didn't understand? Say so. Ask for clarification before moving on.

Move 2

Contextualize

Give your subject the on-ramp. Help them understand where the next question is coming from — what you're curious about and why. This is what makes it feel like a conversation and not a deposition.

Move 3

Ask

A good question is specific enough that your subject knows exactly what you're after — but open enough that they can answer in their own words. Think of it as opening a door to the room you actually want to be in.

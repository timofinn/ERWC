6.02  ·  Nonverbal Cue Bank

Reading the Room

Reference only — nothing to turn in. Pick your cues, keep it natural, and leave the sheet for the classes that come behind you.

How to use this

Round 1 — Listener

Pick 4 cues from anywhere on the chart — don't show your partner. Perform them subtly while they talk. Keep it natural. If they catch you immediately, you went too big.

Round 2 — Speaker

Now you're talking. Pay attention to what you feel as your partner performs their cues. In the debrief, you'll try to name what they did — and what it did to you.

The Goal

See whether your partner can notice a shift and name it afterward. Some of the most powerful cues are the quietest ones. A slow blink lands harder than a loud yawn.

Why this matters for interviewing

Every cue on this chart communicates something — even when you don't mean it to. As an interviewer, you're sending signals constantly: am I interested, distracted, uncomfortable, or fully present? Your subject reads those signals and adjusts. If they sense disengagement, they pull back. If they feel heard, they open up.

The four categories

Disengagement

Signals boredom, distraction, or withdrawal. Speakers notice this and often shorten or flatten their story.

Neutral / Ambiguous

Unclear signal — could mean thinking, distraction, or discomfort. Speakers often second-guess themselves.

Strange / Unexpected

Breaks social norms in ways that create confusion or self-consciousness. Throws speakers off their rhythm.

Engagement

Signals active attention and interest. Speakers open up, elaborate, and feel safe going deeper.

A note on subtlety

The best performers keep it natural. If your partner catches you immediately, you went too big. The goal isn't to distract or annoy — it's to see whether your partner can notice a shift and name it afterward. Some of the most powerful cues are the quietest ones. A slow blink lands harder than a loud yawn.

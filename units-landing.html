Units 4–7

The final four units of the year: exploring consumption, nature, listening, and rhetoric.

Unit 4

Waste More,
Want More

Consumption & consequence

A 7-day curriculum sequence exploring consumption, waste, and their consequences. You'll move from exploring issues that matter to you, through research and advocacy skills, to committing to a project pathway—either a research investigation or an advocacy piece designed to create real impact.

Key Learning Goals
1
Frame consumption and waste problems through a systemic lens—understanding causes, consequences, and who's affected
2
Research credible sources and extract meaningful data to support an argument
3
Craft an inquiry question and working claim that drives either a research project or advocacy piece

Unit 5

Into the
Wild

Ideas, risk, and meaning

Read Jon Krakauer's Into the Wild as a study in how people search for meaning. This is not a judgment on whether Chris McCandless was a hero or an idiot. Instead, we're exploring what he believed, why he acted on those beliefs, and what happens when ideals collide with reality.

Key Learning Goals
1
Understand the philosophical influences on McCandless—Thoreau, Tolstoy, London, Dostoevsky—and the ideas they represented
2
Read for nuance: examine character motivation without rushing to judgment
3
Reflect on your own values and commitments in a time capsule letter addressed to future you

Unit 6

Narrative
Medicine

Listening as a practice

There's an art to listening—not for your turn to speak, but so you can get the fuller picture of someone who trusts you enough to share. This unit challenges you to listen deeply, ask better questions, and conduct a real interview with an elder in your community.

Key Learning Goals
1
Read the room: pick up on body language, tone, and hesitation before someone says it in words
2
Ask questions that go somewhere—moving from surface openers to real depth with authentic presence
3
Conduct, record, annotate, and reflect on a real conversation with an elder subject

Unit 7

Graduation
Speeches

Rhetoric & voice

You're watching five graduation speeches written and delivered by people your age. Your job is to watch like a writer: figure out what they're doing, decide if it works, and figure out what you might steal. Learn the lenses, devices, and moves that make a speech land.

Key Learning Goals
1
Recognize the four lenses: humor-to-wholesome, nostalgia-to-motivational, identity-centered, and risqué-to-critical
2
Master nine rhetorical devices: anaphora, antithesis, metaphor, analogy, chiasmus, hypophora, and more
3
Craft your own graduation speech that deploys at least five devices and reflects your authentic voice

Your Graduation Speech

One lens. Five devices. What Will You Say Before You Go?

Friends, we're coming to the end — both of our time together, and your time in secondary education. Your final project, fittingly enough, calls you to gather your thoughts for one final address.

You wanna talk pretty? It's not magic. This unit we look under the hood at some of the mechanics — some of the time-tested tools orators, musicians, poets, and politicians have used for millennia to captivate audiences with their words. What will yours be?

What You're Building Toward

Write and deliver a graduation speech that commits to one rhetorical lens and deploys at least five of the nine devices covered in class. The speech should feel like you — not a template, not a list of devices, but a real argument made in your voice about something that actually matters to you.

The Nine Devices

Repetition — 7.07
Anaphora The anchor word leads every line.
Antistrophe The anchor word closes every line.
Tricolon Three items. The third lands hardest.
Comparison — 7.08
Antithesis Parallel opposites. The tension is the argument.
Metaphor One thing IS another. No "like" or "as."
Analogy One vehicle, sustained across 3–4 sentences.
Structure — 7.09
Chiasmus A-B becomes B-A. The flip reverses the logic.
Hypophora Ask the question. Answer it yourself.
Parallelism Same structure, repeated. Ideas land as one.

What's Expected

The assignment

Choose one lens — Humor, Nostalgia, Identity, or Critical. Write a speech that commits to it. Use at least five of the nine devices covered in 7.07–7.09. The devices should serve the speech, not the other way around.

You don't need all nine. You need five that feel true to what you're trying to say — and you need to use them well enough that a listener feels them working, even if they don't know what to call them.

The speeches you watched were written by people your age.

The Move: Identity-Centered
What this lens does
The speaker's personal story — their specific identity, their specific struggle — becomes a vehicle for a universal argument. The "I" does the work of "we." The failure mode is when the speech stays in "I" and never makes the leap.

The difference between identity as message vs. identity as evidence

Larissa isn't giving a speech about being undocumented. She's giving a speech about the gap between what the world assumes about you and what's actually true — and she happens to be undocumented, which makes that argument devastating and real. Her identity is the evidence for the claim, not the claim itself. That's the move. If your personal story is the point, you're giving a speech about yourself. If your personal story proves a point, you're giving a speech for everyone in the room.

What to Watch For

  • She uses "Unexpected Reality" as a structural scaffold — three numbered reveals, each bigger than the last. Watch how she controls the pacing of each reveal. Why does #3 come last?
  • Find the moment where the "I" becomes "we." There's a specific line where her personal experience becomes collective — pay attention to how she earns that transition.
  • She's taking a real risk. What has she done earlier in the speech to protect herself before she takes it?
  • Her final line is her argument in one sentence. Find it. It should reframe everything that came before it.

The Move in Action

The Setup — Before any reveal
"Many of you see me standing up here and assume that my life must be pretty great. My parents must be very proud. Nevertheless, I think it's important to note that those are only half-truths. Those are the expectations. My reality has been quite different."
What she's doing here

Before she reveals anything personal, she names the assumption — and she names it in the audience's voice. "Many of you see me and assume..." That's smart. She's not just describing her experience; she's describing the gap between perception and reality that's going to structure the whole speech. The audience is now leaning forward wondering what the reality actually is. She's built suspense before she's told you a single fact about herself.

The Pivot — From "I" to "we"
"Although we do not all share the same struggles, and we haven't gone through the same hurdles throughout life, we do share some of the same sentiments. I know what it's like to be put down, to have your accomplishments go unacknowledged, to feel powerless."
What she's doing here

This is the hinge. She's been telling her story — and she hasn't gotten to the biggest reveal yet — but right here she pauses and says: whatever your version of this is, I've felt it too. She's not claiming everyone has her experience. She's claiming everyone has felt dismissed. That's a much wider net, and it pulls the whole room into her story without erasing the specificity of it.

The Argument — What the whole speech was building to
"We do not have to let expectations become our reality."
What she's doing here

Ten words. That's the thesis. She spent the entire speech proving it was true for her — and then she hands it to everyone in the room. Every person sitting there has something someone assumed about them. Every person has a version of the gap between expectation and reality. She didn't say that. She showed it, and then she named it. That's what identity-centered speeches do when they work: they use the specific to unlock the universal.

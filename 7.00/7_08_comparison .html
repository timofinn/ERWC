All or nothing.
Antithesis Parallel opposites — the tension between them is the argument.
Why it works: Antithesis (an-TITH-uh-sis) shows the power of contrast. Neither half makes sense without the other.
See Some Examples
"Knowledge speaks, wisdom listens."
— Jimi Hendrix
"The only way to deal with an unfree world is to become so absolutely free that your very existence is an act of rebellion."
— Albert Camus
"Success builds character. Failure reveals it."
— Dave Checketts
"If knowledge can create problems, it is not through ignorance that we can solve them."
— Isaac Asimov
Weak vs. Strong
Weak
"Some people give up, and some people keep going."
Strong
"Some people leave a mark. Others leave a mess. The difference is whether you stayed long enough to clean up your own."
Why?
Now there's real tension — and a moral argument buried in the contrast. The parallel structure makes the comparison feel inevitable, not just stated.
Weak
"We worked hard, not easy."
Strong
"We didn't come here to be comfortable. We came here to be changed."
Why?
"Hard vs. easy" is too vague. "Comfortable vs. changed" implies a willingness to be transformed — which is a real argument about what education is for.
Skip to the Assignment
Step 1 — Find an Example

Search for antithesis in a speech, book, headline, or song. Check that the two halves are genuine opposites — not just two different things placed next to each other.

Write down: the example and what the tension between the two sides creates.
Step 2 — Write It

Use the "Not X, but Y" structure. Make sure both halves are genuinely opposed. Then try a second one on a different subject. Two attempts minimum.

Step 3 — Say It

Read it aloud. The two halves should feel like they're pulling against each other. If they don't, sharpen the contrast.

Metaphor A direct statement of identity — one thing IS another.
Why it works: A metaphor is a punch in the gut. It doesn't compare like a simile ("like" or "as") — it collapses the distance entirely. I never met a four I didn't five.
See Some Examples
"Belief is the death of intelligence."
— Robert Anton Wilson
"We are such stuff
As dreams are made on, and our little life
Is rounded with a sleep."
— William Shakespeare, The Tempest, Act 4 Scene 1
Weak vs. Strong
Weak
"Time is money. Don't waste it."
Strong
"Attention is the new currency — and every app on your phone is a pickpocket."
Why?
"Time is money" is invisible from overuse. "Attention is currency" is fresher — and the pickpocket extension adds a specific argument about how tech companies operate. The metaphor does the analysis.
Weak
"The mind is like a computer — input, process, output."
Strong
"The mind is not a hard drive. It's a city — always under construction, half the roads closed, the map outdated the moment you print it."
Why?
Starting with "not a hard drive" rejects the cliché before offering something better. A city is messy, alive, always changing — which is a completely different argument about how thought actually works.
Skip to the Assignment
Step 1 — Find an Example

Find a metaphor that actually surprises you — not a dead cliché. It should make you see something you didn't see before.

Write down: the example and what picture it creates.
Step 2 — Write It

Pick something — anything — and make a direct claim: it IS something else. Not "like" or "as." Push past the first idea that comes to mind. The first idea is usually the cliché. Two attempts minimum.

Step 3 — Say It

Read it aloud. Does the audience see something? If you can't picture it yourself, rewrite it.

Analogy An extended comparison — one vehicle, sustained across 3–4 sentences.
Why it works: Logical momentum. Secure the premise, and the conclusion becomes inevitable. It's a straight line from "I'm pickin' up what you're puttin' down" to "Shut up and take my money."
See Some Examples
"Happiness is like a butterfly: the more you chase it, the more it will elude you. But if you turn your attention to other things, it will come and sit softly on your shoulder."
— Henry David Thoreau
"Holding on to anger is like grasping a hot coal with the intent of throwing it at someone else — you are the one who gets burned."
— Siddhartha Gautama
"A sentence should contain no unnecessary words... for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts."
— William Strunk
"Having children makes you no more a parent than having a piano makes you a pianist."
— Michael Levine
"The means to gain happiness is to throw out from oneself like a spider in all directions an adhesive web of love, and to catch in it all that comes."
— Leo Tolstoy
"It is with words as with sunbeams — the more they are condensed, the deeper they burn."
— Robert Southey
Weak vs. Strong
Weak
"We're like athletes who've been training — but also like students who've been studying, and workers who've been preparing."
Strong
"A musician practices the same phrase a thousand times — not to perform it a thousand times, but so that when the moment comes, the hands know what to do without asking the brain. That's what four years of this has been. The concert starts now."
Why?
Stacking three different comparisons dilutes all of them — nothing sticks. One vehicle, sustained to a payoff, beats three weak ones every time.
Weak
"Life is like a race — you have to run fast. It's also like a garden — you have to tend it. The point is, work hard."
Strong
"Think of a chef who spends years in culinary school. They learn every technique. They practice every dish. They burn things, undersalt things, start over. But they don't open the restaurant until they're ready. We've been in culinary school. Tonight is opening night."
Why?
One vehicle — the chef — held all the way through. The final line closes the analogy with a payoff that only works because the whole thing built toward it.

Step 1 — Find an Example

Find an analogy that builds over multiple sentences. It should feel like an argument, not a decoration.

Write down: the example and what argument it's making.
Step 2 — Write It

Pick one vehicle and commit to it across 3–4 sentences. Don't switch. The argument should follow naturally from the comparison by the final sentence. Two attempts minimum.

Step 3 — Say It

Read it aloud. Does the comparison hold from sentence to sentence? If it drifts, that's exactly where you revise.

Master Class See them woven together in one excerpt.

One passage. Multiple devices working at the same time. This is what it looks like when a writer isn't reaching for a device — they're just thinking hard and the devices follow naturally. Read it once straight through. Then open the breakdowns below.

"Nature loves courage. You make the commitment and nature will respond to that commitment by removing impossible obstacles. Dream the impossible dream and the world will not grind you under, it will lift you up. This is the trick. This is what all these teachers and philosophers who really counted, who really touched the alchemical gold, this is what they understood. This is the shamanic dance in the waterfall. This is how magic is done. By hurling yourself into the abyss and discovering it's a feather bed."
— Terence McKenna
Anaphora — "This is..."

Five successive clauses open with "This is." That's the anchor — and it's doing two things at once: building rhythmic momentum and pointing. Each repetition narrows the focus, stacking certainty on top of certainty until the final image lands.

"This is the trick.
This is what all these teachers and philosophers... understood.
This is the shamanic dance in the waterfall.
This is how magic is done."

By the fifth "This is," you're not asking what it means. You already believe it. That's the anaphora doing its job.

Antithesis — "Grind you under / Lift you up"

Two opposing outcomes, placed side by side in parallel structure. The contrast doesn't just describe — it argues. Commitment changes which outcome you get.

"...the world will not grind you under, it will lift you up."

Neither half makes full sense without the other. The gap between them is where the argument lives.

Metaphor & Analogy — The Abyss and the Feather Bed

This is metaphor doing philosophical work — and it's the line that makes the whole passage land. The abyss is the ultimate fear. The feather bed is the ultimate comfort. One image collapses both into a single act: the leap.

"By hurling yourself into the abyss and discovering it's a feather bed."

Notice that McKenna doesn't explain the metaphor. He trusts it to do the work. That's what a good metaphor does — it hands the audience a picture and lets them live in it.

The "alchemical gold" and "shamanic dance in the waterfall" work the same way — high-stakes imagery that doesn't need unpacking because the images themselves carry the meaning.

What Makes This Work As a Whole

McKenna isn't deploying devices deliberately — he's thinking out loud, and the devices follow from the thinking. That's the goal for your speech too. You don't stitch devices onto ideas. You find ideas worth saying, and then you use the devices to make them land harder.

Notice the structure of the whole passage:

Logical claim → Nature loves courage.
Antithesis → Grind you under / lift you up.
Anaphora → This is... This is... This is...
Metaphor → The abyss / the feather bed.

Each device hands off to the next. The logic earns the emotion. The emotion earns the image. That's what good rhetoric feels like from the inside — not a checklist, but a conversation that builds.

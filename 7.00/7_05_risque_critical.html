Two Speakers. One Lens.

Larkin Richards + Nataly Buhr

The Lens: Risqué → Critical
What this lens does
This lens is about naming the elephant in the room. The speaker uses the podium as a platform to shine light on the systemic or institutional problems most classmates know of, but are too polite or mild-mannered to say.

Make no mistake: the risk is real, as are the consequences (like making the news). But what separates this from a standard-issue gripe session is the design behind it: Trust me first, then let me tell you all the things that are wrong about this place.

The Logic Behind the Lens

Notice the move here: neither speaker comes right out the gate swinging. They bide their time — spending the opening lines either thanking people or proving themselves trustworthy. The point is to establish that they're part of the tribe before they start throwing haymakers. There's design behind that. It's the line between blubbery whining and a credible critique.

What to Watch For — Both Speakers

  • Both speakers build credibility before the critique. Find exactly where each one decides to turn — and what they've established before that moment.
  • Pay attention to specificity. Larkin names a system. Nataly names individuals. What does that difference cost each of them?
  • Larkin's mic gets cut. Does that help or hurt his argument? Think about what the interruption itself communicates.
  • Nataly uses the "thank you" format as a Trojan horse. Find where the gratitude turns — and what effect the contrast creates.

Larkin Richards

Wyomissing Area Jr/Sr High School, Class of 2014 — Pennsylvania. Class president four years running. Mic cut before he finished.

The Buildup — Earning the right to critique
Before the turn
"Throughout my time at Wyomissing Area, I have pursued every leadership opportunity available to me. In addition to being a member of student council since I was a freshman, my classmates have also elected me class president for the past four years — which has been my greatest honor."
What he's doing here

He's not bragging. He's establishing standing. Four years of class president means he's been inside the system longer and more deeply than almost anyone else in that gymnasium. When he turns and says the system doesn't actually work, he's the most credible person in the room to say it. You can't dismiss him as someone who didn't try — he tried harder than anyone.

The Critique — Before the mic cuts
"However, at our school, the title of class president could more accurately be described as 'class party planner,' and student council's main obligation is to paint signs every week."
What he's doing here

The word "However" is doing a lot. Everything before it was gratitude and credibility. Everything after it is the actual speech. Notice that he's not naming a person — he's naming a system, a structure, a failure of the institution. That's a more defensible critique. It's also more interesting: he's not angry at anyone, he's disappointed in what was possible and wasn't done. Then the mic goes out. Which, honestly, proves his point.

Nataly Buhr

San Ysidro High School, Class of 2019 — San Diego, CA. Valedictorian. Departed from the pre-approved script. School officials called it "inappropriate and out of line."

The Setup — Real gratitude first
The genuine "thank yous"
"To my mother and father: you are the most hardworking and caring people that I know... To the teachers — Madame Rosa, Ms. Mulligan, Ms. Gomez, Mr. Trevino, Mr. Solomon, and Mr. Lamont — thank you for being so invested in your students' education and well-being."
What she's doing here

She names names. Specific names, real people, genuine gratitude. That specificity matters — it signals to the audience that she's paying attention, that she knows the difference between who showed up and who didn't. When she shifts later and names names in a different way, it lands harder because you already know she means what she says when she singles someone out.

The Turn — Same format, different target
"To my counselor: thank you for teaching me to fend for myself. You were consistently unavailable to my parents and me, despite scheduled appointments. Only in these past few weeks, with award ceremonies and graduation approaching, did you begin making your appearance."
What she's doing here

She's using the exact same "thank you" structure — but the content has inverted completely. The form is polite; the content is devastating. That gap is what creates the effect. She's not yelling. She's not losing control. She's being precise and specific and icily grateful. That restraint makes it more powerful, not less. The crowd's reaction tells you everything about whether it worked.

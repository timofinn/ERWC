Before You Structure

Know Your Clock

A graduation speech is not an essay. It has a hard stop. If you run long, people don't listen; they just look at their phones and wish you were done. Here is where your time goes:

Section Time Limit The Core Job
Opening 30–45 sec A hook and one line that sets your angle.
Body 3–5 min Two or three story beats, the turn, and the lesson.
Closing 30–45 sec A callback and a send-off that lands clean.
Clear These Off the Table

The List You Don't Want to Be On

Five ways graduation speeches lose the room. Check your ideas against them before you outline.

  • ✕ Inside jokes. You are not talking to your five closest friends. The whole class and their families are in that room. If only three people get it, cut it.
  • ✕ Going over time. Nothing kills goodwill faster than running long. If your outline does not fit five minutes, cut it now. Do not wait for the stage.
  • ✕ Too much "I." Your story is the vehicle, not the destination. The speech must belong to everyone in the room.
  • ✕ Reading the whole thing. You can use notes. You must not disappear into a piece of paper. Look at people. Breathe. Talk to them.
  • ✕ Forcing inspiration. You do not need to give a TED talk. A specific, honest moment lands harder than borrowed wisdom that does not fit your voice.

What makes an outline work

An outline is not a draft. It is a map. You are not writing sentences; you are staking out territory. Each section should be specific enough that a stranger could read it and know exactly what your speech is about. If it could describe anyone's graduation speech, rewrite it.

The test for specificity

Weak outlines use categories: "I'll talk about a hard time sophomore year." Strong outlines name the scene: "The day I failed the AP exam and had to call my dad from the parking lot." The second one has a movie in it. The first one has nothing.

The turn is the whole speech

Every section points toward the turn. This is the moment the story stops being about you and starts being about the room. If you cannot locate your turn in the outline, you do not have a speech yet. You have a diary entry.

Lens: Humor → Wholesome

Opening Hook
≈ 30–45 seconds
"I want to start by apologizing to Mr. Kim for what happened to his classroom turtle in March 2022. I won't say more. He knows."
Why it works: Immediate laugh, names a specific person, creates a mystery the audience wants resolved. Sets a tone of honesty without being heavy.
Body — Story Beat 1
3–5 minutes total
The moment: Junior year, period 3 AP Chemistry. The day four of us decided to collectively pretend we had done the lab; we proceeded to make up confident data for 40 minutes while Ms. Flores watched with pure professional pity.

What it carries: The gap between the self we performed and the self we actually were. We were faking it, and most of us were faking it in different ways all four years.
The Turn
≈ 30 seconds
"I thought that was my story; the kid who bluffed his way through Chemistry. But look around. We all had a class, a test, a moment where we had no idea what we were doing and just kept moving anyway. That's not embarrassing. That's actually the whole job."
Closing
≈ 30–45 seconds
Callback to the turtle: "Mr. Kim, I still don't know how it got out. But I think about that turtle a lot. It got out of its tank, it had no plan, and it made it to the hallway. That's all of us today."
Echoes the hook. The joke turns into a landmark. The room gets to laugh and feel something at the same time.

Lens: Identity-Centered

Opening Hook
≈ 30–45 seconds
"The first time someone asked me where I was really from, I was in 7th grade. I said my zip code. That was the wrong answer, apparently."
Why it works: Specific age, specific moment, and a small contradiction that holds the whole tension of the speech in one sentence. Doesn't over-explain. Trusts the audience.
Body — Story Beat 1
3–5 minutes total
The moment: Freshman orientation. The part where they asked everyone to raise their hand if they spoke a second language at home; half the room went quiet and kept their hands down. Including me.

What it carries: There is a person you pretend to be in the hallway and the person you are at home. I spent four years trying to figure out which one was real.
Body — Story Beat 2
Optional
The moment: Senior year, the day I did the college essay in two languages because I could not figure out which one sounded like me.

What it adds: The choice became impossible to avoid. It widens the lens because many people in this room had to decide which face to put on paper.
The Turn
≈ 30 seconds
"I'm not the only person in this room who kept part of themselves in the hallway. We all had something we decided wasn't safe to bring through the front door. Today I'm saying: bring it in. There's more room than they told us."
Closing
≈ 30–45 seconds
Callback to the zip code line: "I know where I'm really from now. And so do you. Take it with you."
Short. Echoes the hook. Turns the opening question into a statement. Done.
Build the Outline

Section by Section

Use what you dug up in 7.11. Do not write full sentences yet. Just map the moves. You are staking out territory, not decorating it.

1. Opening Hook
≈ 30–45 seconds

One strong move. A question, a line, a scene, or a contradiction that makes the room lean in. Don't explain it yet. Just land it.

My hook
Why it works
Weak vs. Strong  ·  Hook
Weak
"Today is a day we will all remember forever."
Strong
"The last time I sat in this gym, I was crying because we lost the semifinals by one point; then Mr. Garza gave me a granola bar without saying anything."
Why?
The weak version is a generic script. The strong version names a specific place, a loss, and a person. It creates immediate mystery and sets up a story.
Weak
"High school has been quite a journey for all of us."
Strong
"I changed my major plan four times in four years. The last choice involved marine biology. I have never been to the ocean."
Why?
The weak phrase says nothing. The strong line is specific and self-aware. It earns a laugh and signals an honest speech.

3–5 minutes total for all beats

4. The Turn
The pivot point — ≈ 30 seconds

This is where "I" becomes "we." Flip the lens from your life to the room. If you are stuck, ask: What did this teach me that is also true for the person sitting next to me?

The turn
Weak vs. Strong  ·  The Turn
Weak
"And I think we can all learn something from this. Life is hard, but we get through it together."
Strong
"I spent two years thinking I was the only person in this building who didn't have a plan. Then I asked around. Turns out half of you were also making it up. We were all improvising."
Why?
The weak option is a bumper sticker slogan. The strong option names a specific false belief and instantly opens it up to the room.
5. Closing
≈ 30–45 seconds

A callback to your hook, or a clean send-off that ends on a sharp image. Do not write "in conclusion" or "thank you." The last thing they hear should feel like a door opening.

The closing move
The echo
Weak vs. Strong  ·  Closing
Weak
"Thank you to my teachers and parents. Good luck to the class of 2026. We did it."
Strong
"Mr. Kim, I still don't know how that turtle got loose. But it had no instructions, no map, and it made it out anyway. Don't look back. Walk out the door."
Why?
The weak close is white noise; it signals to the audience that they can clap now. The strong version circles back to the joke from the opening hook and transforms it into a clean, sharp landing order.
Before You Leave

The Reality Check

Swap outlines with a partner. Three minutes each. Do not sit there and tell your partner their outline is "good." A true friend tells you when you have food caught in your teeth. Find the food.

You are an outside reader. You cannot see inside their brain. If a section feels boring or confusing, point right at it. Ask these exact questions:

  • Where did you lose me? Point to the exact spot where your mind wandered or the logic lost its grip.
  • Is this a real scene? If they wrote something vague like "I talk about failure," tell them to scrap it and pick a real, physical moment.
  • Where is the turn? Can you see the exact line where the speech stops being about them and starts being about the whole room? If not, they are talking to themselves.
  • Did they flag any common mistakes? If you see an inside joke or a generic quote, call it out and kill it.

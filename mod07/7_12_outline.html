3–5 min

Clear These Off the Table

The List You Don't Want to Be On

These are the five most common ways graduation speeches lose the room. Check yours against them before you outline.

  • ✕ Inside jokes. You're not talking to your five closest friends. The whole class — and their families — are in that room.
  • ✕ Going over time. Nothing kills goodwill faster than running long. If your outline doesn't fit 5 minutes, cut it now, not on the day.
  • ✕ Too much "I." Your story is the vehicle, not the destination. The speech should feel like it belongs to everyone in the room.
  • ✕ Reading the whole thing. You can have notes. You should not disappear into a paper. Make eye contact. Breathe. Talk to people.
  • ✕ Forcing inspiration. You don't have to deliver a TED talk. A specific, honest moment lands harder than borrowed wisdom that doesn't fit your voice.
Build the Outline

Your Speech, Section by Section

Use what you dug up in 7.11. Don't write sentences yet — just map the moves. You're staking out territory, not decorating it.

Opening Hook
≈ 30–45 seconds

One strong move — a question, a line, a scene, a contradiction — that makes the room lean in. Don't explain it yet. Just land it.

My hook
Why it works (one sentence)
Body — Story Beat 1
3–5 minutes total for all beats

A specific moment or scene from your brainstorm. Anchor it in time and place — not "sophomore year was hard" but "the day I walked into Mr. Reyes's class and everyone was silent."

The moment (one or two sentences)
What it says (what does this beat carry?)
Body — Story Beat 2
Optional — use if you have a strong second scene

A second scene that adds tension, raises the stakes, or widens the lens to the whole class.

The moment
What it adds
The Turn
The pivot point — maybe 30 seconds

This is where "I" becomes "we." The moment your story opens up and speaks to everyone. If you're stuck, ask: What did this teach me that's also true for everyone else in this room?

The turn (one or two sentences)
Closing
≈ 30–45 seconds

Callback to your hook, or a clean send-off that ends on a specific image or line — not "in conclusion." The last thing they hear should feel like a door opening, not a summary.

The closing move
Does it echo the hook? (yes/no + how)
Before You Leave Today

Partner Check

Swap outlines with someone near you. You have three minutes each. Your job is not to fix their speech — it's to tell them what you saw and what you missed.

  • Can you describe their hook in one sentence? (If not, it's not clear enough yet.)
  • Is there a real, specific moment in the body — or just a vague concept?
  • Does the turn make the shift from "I" to "we"? Point to the line where it happens.
  • Does the close land, or does it just stop?
  • Flag anything on the Common Mistakes list that shows up.

The Move: Nostalgia → Motivational
What this lens does
The speaker walks the audience backward through shared memory — not to dwell there, but to build weight. By the time they turn toward the future, the past has earned the pivot. The nostalgia isn't warmth for its own sake. It's the setup.

The risk: nostalgia about yourself vs. nostalgia for everyone

The failure mode of this lens is a speech that lists your personal memories and expects everyone else to care. What makes Roohan's version work is that he goes wide — Beyblades, fidget spinners, the floor-is-lava game — things everyone in that gym lived through together. It's not his memory. It's the class's memory. That distinction is everything. If you try this lens in your own speech, the memories have to be shared, not personal.

What to Watch For

  • He structures the nostalgia in phases: kindergarten → middle school → high school → pandemic. Watch how each phase accelerates — and how March 13, 2020 hits differently because of everything that came before it.
  • Find the exact line where the speech turns from looking back to pushing forward. It's not gradual — there's a specific moment.
  • What does he do with the pandemic? He doesn't treat it as a tragedy to overcome. Pay attention to how he frames it.
  • Notice the ratio: how long does he spend in the past before he makes his argument about the future?

The Move in Action

The Nostalgia — Building the shared past
"Those were the days when our biggest fear was losing the floor-is-lava game during recess. The days when we showed off our Silly Bandz, traded Pokemon cards, and battled with Beyblades. When we watched Ben 10 and iCarly late into the night."
What he's doing here

Every single item on that list is chosen to be universally recognizable — not "my" memory, but "our" memory. He's doing inventory the same way Chase does, but the goal is different: he's not going for laughs, he's going for recognition. He wants the whole room — including the parents — to nod. That collective nod is what lets him say "we" for the rest of the speech and have it land honestly.

The Wall — March 13, 2020
"And then came Friday, March 13th, 2020. The day our high school journey took a sharp turn."
What he's doing here

Everything about this sentence is deliberate. He gives the exact date — not "when the pandemic started," not "when school closed," but that specific Friday. Anyone who lived through it knows exactly where they were. The phrase "sharp turn" is doing structural work: it signals to the audience that the speech is also turning. The nostalgia section is over. Something else is beginning.

The Reframe — How he turns the pandemic into the argument
"Remember that your success isn't defined by the opportunities you're given — it's defined by what you make of the opportunities you have."
What he's doing here

This is where the pandemic stops being a detour and becomes the point. He's not saying "despite the pandemic, you succeeded." He's saying the pandemic is the proof. The whole nostalgia section — all that shared history — was building to this: you've already proven you can make something out of what you're handed. That's a much stronger argument than "go seize the day," because the class just watched themselves do it.

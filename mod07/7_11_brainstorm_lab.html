2
Mine the Material
Work all three prompts. Don't filter. Volume now, quality later.
Prompt A — The Funny List

List 5–7 things from this year (or your school career) that were genuinely absurd, awkward, or ridiculous. The more specific, the better. No one needs to hear "lunch was chaotic." Give us the incident.

  • A moment that was embarrassing in the best possible way
  • Something that went completely wrong and became a story
  • A rule, tradition, or ritual that makes no sense if you actually think about it
  • A teacher or classmate moment that the whole class still references
  • The one thing that was universally, reliably frustrating — but also kind of funny in retrospect
Prompt B — Find the Turn

A Humor → Wholesome speech lives and dies on its pivot. Somewhere in your funny material, there's a genuine feeling hiding underneath. Answer this:

The freewrite

Take one item from your list above. Write for 3 minutes without stopping: what was actually going on underneath that moment? What did it reveal about who you were, who these people are, or what this place meant to you? Don't be cute. Be honest.

The turn question

In one sentence: what is the joke actually about at its core? What does it say about growing up, about this class, about being 18 and figuring things out?

Prompt C — The Heart Under the Joke

Most forgettable graduation speeches are either all laughs (no landing) or all sincerity (no hook). Your job is the pivot. Finish these sentences — don't overthink them:

  • The thing we laughed about most was ___, but what it was really about was ___.
  • We complained about ___ constantly, and honestly, I'm going to miss it.
  • There's one person in this room who ___, and I don't think they know how much that mattered.
Prompt A — The Memory List

List 5–7 shared memories — things the class or school experienced together. The more specific, the stronger. "Prom" is a category. "The moment the DJ played that song and everyone lost it" is a memory.

  • A moment everyone in the room was present for — a game, a protest, a performance, a disaster
  • A before-and-after: something that changed the class's mood or trajectory
  • A small, specific detail that captures what freshman year actually felt like
  • The thing that's different about your class compared to the ones before you
  • A tradition, a running joke, a phrase — something that belongs to your class specifically
Prompt B — The Setup / Payoff

Nostalgia → Motivational only works if the past feels like setup, not just warmup. The memory has to point somewhere.

The freewrite

Pick one memory from your list. Write for 3 minutes: what did you not understand then that you understand now? What did that moment teach you that you couldn't have named at the time?

The bridge question

In one sentence: what does looking back at that moment make you want to do next? That's your call to action. That's your payoff.

Prompt C — Then and Now

The best nostalgic speeches hold two time frames at once: who we were and who we became. Finish these:

  • When we started, we thought ___ mattered. Now we know it was really about ___.
  • The version of me that walked in freshman year would not believe ___.
  • What held us together through ___ was ___. That's worth keeping.
Prompt A — Name What's Different

Identity-Centered speeches only work if the "I" earns its way to "we." Start by getting honest about what makes your experience specific — then we'll figure out what it connects to.

  • Something about your background, identity, or story that shapes how you moved through this school
  • A moment where your difference was visible — not necessarily painful, but noticeable
  • Something you had to figure out that most people in the room didn't have to think about
  • A way you changed yourself to fit in — and whether it was worth it
  • Something you know, because of who you are, that this room needs to hear
Prompt B — The Universal Under the Specific

The "I" has to do broad work. Your story isn't just about you — it's evidence of something true for everyone.

The freewrite

Pick one item from your list. Write for 3 minutes: what does this experience say about something bigger — about this school, this generation, this country, or what it means to grow up right now? Don't be modest. Make the claim.

The universal question

Complete this: "I'm not the only one in this room who has felt ___." That's where your "I" becomes "we."

Prompt C — What You Want Them to Walk Away Knowing

Your speech is an argument. It's making a case for something. Finish these:

  • I spent too long thinking ___ was a weakness. It was actually ___.
  • The thing I wish someone had told me freshman year is ___.
  • What I know about ___ [your specific identity/experience] that applies to everyone in this room: ___.
Prompt A — Name the Elephant

The Risqué → Critical lens only works if you go somewhere real. List the things everyone knows but nobody says out loud at graduation. Be specific — vague complaints land flat.

  • Something about this school that genuinely doesn't work — and everyone knows it
  • A contradiction: what this place says it values vs. what it actually rewards
  • Something students were told that turned out to be wrong, misleading, or just absurd
  • A system, rule, or expectation that treated students like children when they weren't
  • What graduating actually means — honest answer, not the brochure version
Prompt B — Earn the Trust First

You can't open with the critique. The audience has to like you first — or they'll hear the criticism as bitterness. You need a hook that makes them laugh or nod before you name the problem.

The freewrite

Write for 3 minutes: what do you genuinely appreciate about this place, these people, or this experience — something real, not a platitude? You need something true to stand on before you take a swing.

The trust question

In one sentence: what do you and this audience agree on — the thing that makes you one of them — before you ask them to think critically?

Prompt C — The Critique With a Point

Naming what's wrong isn't enough. The best critical speeches end with something to do with the critique — a challenge, a standard, a different story. Finish these:

  • We were told ___, and most of us believed it. Here's what was actually true: ___.
  • This place got ___ wrong. The version I wish it had been: ___.
  • If the next class walking through those doors got one thing different, I'd want it to be ___.

3
Triage
You've got raw material. Now cut it down to what's actually worth building on.
Circle your three best lines

Go back through everything you wrote. Look for the moments where you said something real — specific, honest, surprising. Not the line you're proud of. The line that feels a little risky to say out loud. That's the one.

Three tests

Is it specific? (A name, a place, a moment — not a category.)
Is it true? (Does it actually represent your experience?)
Does it connect outward? (Would someone else in the room recognize it?)

Write your three best lines in your handout packet, labeled "my material."

4
Find the Device Hook
One of your three lines is ready to become a rhetorical device. Here's how to spot it.
Match your material to a device

Look at your three lines and ask: which of these has a shape already hiding in it?

Repetition devices (7.07)

If one of your lines has an anchor word or phrase that could lead or close three parallel ideas — that's anaphora or antistrophe.
If you have three parallel items and the third is the heaviest — that's a tricolon.

Comparison devices (7.08)

If your line sets two things against each other — a before and after, a contradiction, a cost — that's antithesis.
If you're describing one thing as if it's something else entirely — that's a metaphor or analogy.

Structure devices (7.09)

If you want to flip an idea on itself — A-B becomes B-A — that's chiasmus.
If you want to ask a question you already know how to answer — that's hypophora.
If you have a list of parallel actions or commitments — that's parallelism.

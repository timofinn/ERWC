The Four Lenses

Every speech this week fits at least one of these formats. Knowing the format before you watch is what separates a writer watching from an audience member just sitting there.

7.02

Humor → Wholesome

Win them with laughs, awe them with heart. The joke is the knock on the door — pull off the punchline, they let you in.

7.03

Nostalgia → Motivational

Walks the audience through shared memory, then turns it into a call to move forward. The past has to feel like setup, not just warmup.

7.04

Identity-Centered

Personal difference becomes universal insight. The "I" has to do broad work — or it's just a speech about yourself.

7.05

Risqué → Critical

Name the elephant in the room. Trust the audience first, then tell them everything that's wrong about this place.

The Nine Devices — A Quick Reference

You'll need at least five of these in your final speech. Here's the cheat sheet.

Repetition — 7.07
Anaphora The anchor word leads every line.
Antistrophe The anchor word closes every line.
Tricolon Three items. The third lands hardest.
Comparison — 7.08
Antithesis Parallel opposites. The tension is the argument.
Metaphor One thing IS another. No "like" or "as."
Analogy One vehicle, sustained across 3–4 sentences.
Structure — 7.09
Chiasmus A-B becomes B-A. The flip reverses the logic.
Hypophora Ask the question. Answer it yourself.
Parallelism Same structure, repeated. Ideas land as one.

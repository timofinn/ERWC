Devices of Repetition

Anaphora · Antistrophe · Tricolon

Notebook Route

Full credit on completion.

Performance Route

All or nothing.
Anaphora Beginning repetition — the anchor word leads every line.
Why it works: The piece you repeat carries the emotional payload of the whole piece. Make it punchy.
See Some Examples
In Speeches & Writing
"Where there is discord, may we bring harmony. Where there is error, may we bring truth. Where there is doubt, may we bring faith."
— Margaret Thatcher, Downing Street, 1979
"We shall fight on the beaches. We shall fight on the landing grounds. We shall fight in the fields and in the streets."
— Winston Churchill, June 1940
"I have a dream that one day this nation will rise up. I have a dream that my four little children will one day live in a nation where they will not be judged by the color of their skin. I have a dream today."
— Martin Luther King Jr., August 1963
"It takes strength to love, but it takes a specialized brand of magic to believe that the world can be better than it is. It takes more than just hope; it takes a refusal to be broken."
— Original
In Music — Pull These Up and Listen
"Every Breath You Take" — The Police (1983)
Written by Sting
"Every breath you take / Every move you make / Every bond you break / Every step you take / I'll be watching you." The anchor "every" turns what sounds like a love song into something far more unsettling. Notice how the repeated opener reframes each line that follows.
"Yesterday" — The Beatles (1965)
Written by Paul McCartney
McCartney opens each verse with a single word — and that word is doing everything. Longing, loss, the gap between then and now. The anchor IS the thesis of the song. Count how many times a line begins with it.
"Closing Time" — Semisonic (1998)
Written by Dan Wilson
On the surface it's a bar closing. The anchor phrase earns a second meaning by the end — leaving a place you've outgrown, stepping into something new. Count how many times the phrase opens a new line. What does it mean by the final verse?
"Ain't No Mountain High Enough" — Marvin Gaye & Tammi Terrell (1967)
Written by Nickolas Ashford & Valerie Simpson
The title phrase is the anchor — repeated, built upon, returned to. Each repetition adds a new obstacle that gets dismissed. By the end it's not just a promise. It's a vow.
Weak vs. Strong
Weak
"I think we can do better. I think we should try harder. I think we owe it to ourselves."
Strong
"We owe it to the ones who didn't make it here. We owe it to the teachers who stayed late and never said a word about it. We owe it to every earlier version of ourselves who wasn't sure we could."
Why?
"I think" carries nothing. "We owe it" carries debt, responsibility, gratitude. The anchor has to mean something before the repetition can build on it.
Weak
"There are things we need to change. There are things we need to fix. There are things we need to do."
Strong
"We inherited a climate in crisis. We inherited a political system nobody under thirty designed. We inherited every problem the generation before us was too comfortable to solve."
Why?
"We inherited" is specific, charged, and slightly accusatory — it implies someone left something behind. "There are things" is filler. The anchor does the argument.
Skip to the Assignment
1
Name the feeling first

Before you write a word: what do you want the audience to feel when this lands? Write it down. One word. That's your target.

2
Find the anchor — try three, reject two

Write three possible anchor phrases. Cross out the first one — it's probably the cliché. Cross out the weakest of the remaining two. Circle the one that carries the most weight on its own, before any repetition. "I am grateful" carries nothing. "We owe it" carries debt. That's the difference.

3
Draft three lines — each adds specific evidence

Open every line with your anchor. After the anchor, give one specific piece of evidence: a person, a moment, a fact. Generic lines waste the anchor. Save your most specific, most emotionally loaded line for last. Do this twice — two drafts, two different anchors.

4
Read it aloud — check the weight

Say it out loud to a partner. One question: does the anchor feel heavier by the third line, or does it just repeat? If it just repeats, either the anchor is wrong or the lines aren't specific enough. Revise once.

The test

Your partner answers yes or no: did the anchor earn more weight each time it came back? No = find more specific evidence for each line. Yes = you're done.

Sketchnote It

In your notebook, draw what anaphora looks like. Think: three lines, each starting at the same point on the left, then branching out to the right with different content. Label the anchor. Show the evidence growing. Use arrows, icons, whatever makes the structure visible to you.

Antistrophe Ending repetition — the anchor word closes every line.
Why it works: If anaphora sets up for takeoff, antistrophe sticks the landing. Like anaphora, choose your repeated phrase wisely — it's what echoes in the listener's mind.
See Some Examples
In Speeches & Writing
"...government of the people, by the people, for the people."
— Abraham Lincoln, Gettysburg Address, 1863
"People are illogical, unreasonable, and self-centered. Love them anyway. If you are successful, you will win some false friends and some true enemies. Succeed anyway. The good you do today will be forgotten tomorrow. Do good anyway."
— Kent M. Keith, "The Paradoxical Commandments," 1968
"If you do not choose your path, the world will choose it for you. If you do not define your worth, the market will define it for you. If you do not guard your time, your distractions will guard it for you."
— Original example
In Music — Pull This Up and Listen
"Single Ladies (Put a Ring on It)" — Beyoncé (2008)
Written by Beyoncé, Terius Nash, Thaddis Harrell, Christopher Stewart
The hook closes line after line with the same phrase — and that closing phrase is the verdict. Track how many different situations get punctuated by the same closer. By the third or fourth time, you're not just hearing it. You're agreeing with it.
Weak vs. Strong
Weak
"They questioned us. They doubted us. They underestimated us."
Strong
"They questioned us — and we kept going anyway. They doubted us — and we kept going anyway. They underestimated us — and we kept going anyway."
Why?
"Us" is just the object of a verb. "Anyway" is defiance. Every time it closes a line, it reframes everything before it as an obstacle that got cleared.
Weak
"The law said no. The system said no. The world said no."
Strong
"The law said she couldn't vote. The system said she couldn't vote. The world said she couldn't vote. She organized until the Constitution said she could."
Why?
"Vote" is specific, consequential, historically charged. Closing on it three times builds the injustice. The final flip to "could" makes the resolution feel earned.
Skip to the Assignment
1
Name the feeling first

What do you want the audience to feel when each line lands? The closing anchor is what echoes — so name the emotional target before you pick the word.

2
Find the closing anchor — try three, reject two

Write three candidates for your closing word or phrase. Look for something that signals resolution, defiance, or verdict. "Anyway." "Still." "For us." "Vote." Cross out the flat one. Cross out the vague one. Circle the one that stings. It should be able to close three completely different lines without wearing out.

3
Draft three lines — each arrives at the same destination

Write three lines with different content — different situations, different people, different stakes — that all end with your anchor. The contrast between lines is what makes the repeated closing feel like a verdict rather than a pattern. Do this twice with two different closing words.

4
Read it aloud — check the echo

Say it out loud to a partner. One question: by the third time the closing word arrives, does it feel like a verdict — or just a repetition? If it's just a repetition, the lines before it are too similar. Vary the content more, then try again.

The test

Your partner answers: verdict or repetition? Repetition = your three lines aren't different enough from each other. Find more contrast between them.

Sketchnote It

Draw antistrophe in your notebook. Think: three lines converging from the left, all arriving at the same point on the right. Show the different content on the left side. Show the shared anchor landing on the right. Label what makes each line different — and what stays the same.

Tricolon The Rule of Three — crescendo required.
Why it works: For some reason our brains like things in three. Make the last one count — longer, more specific, more emotionally loaded than the first two. Items one and two are the setup. Save your heaviest hitter for last.
See Some Examples
Examples in Action
"Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness."
— Declaration of Independence, 1776
"I have the ambition of a CEO, the schedule of a world leader, and the bank account of a Victorian orphan."
— Original example
"It's the hope of slaves sitting around a fire singing freedom songs; the hope of immigrants setting out for distant shores; the hope of a young naval lieutenant bravely patrolling the Mekong Delta."
— Barack Obama, 2004 DNC Keynote
"We lost sleep over it. We lost friends over it. We lost years we're never getting back."
— Original example
The Anti-Tricolon — What to Avoid

The most common mistake: putting the heavy item first. The sentence has nowhere to go, and items two and three feel like a letdown.

✗ "We leave behind our legacy, our lockers, and our old teachers."
Legacy — the heaviest word — lands first. Everything after it deflates.
✓ Fix it
"We leave behind our lockers, we leave behind our teachers, and we leave behind a legacy that will outlast our time here."
Now the weight builds. The heaviest item lands last, where it belongs.
Weak vs. Strong
Weak
"We are young, smart, and ready."
Strong
"We are young, we are sharp, and we are finally ready to speak for ourselves."
Why?
The third item grows — more syllables, more specificity. The sentence feels complete because the breath runs out at exactly the right moment.
Weak
"I remember the tests, the sports, and the friends."
Strong
"I remember the panic of the tests, the adrenaline of the games, and the quiet moments in the hallway that made it all worth it."
Why?
The third item expands into a full scene. The emotional landing is earned because the first two set it up — and the extra length signals: this one matters most.
Weak
"We have the tools, the time, and the goal."
Strong
"We have the tools, we have the time, and we have a world that is waiting for us to fix it."
Why?
The repeated "we have" builds a drumbeat — anaphora inside a tricolon. The third item breaks the expected rhythm with a bigger claim, and the sentence earns its ending.
Skip to the Assignment
1
Name the feeling first

What do you want the audience to feel when the third item lands? The whole sentence builds to that moment — so know where you're going before you write item one.

2
Write item three first

Start at the end. Write the most specific, most emotionally loaded thing you want to say. If it fits in five words, it isn't heavy enough yet — expand it until it costs something to say. That's your item three. Items one and two are the setup; write them after.

3
Draft the full tricolon — then flip it deliberately

Write items one and two: shorter, simpler, leading toward item three. Read the full sequence. Then write the anti-tricolon: put item three first, items one and two after. Read that version too. The collapse in momentum is the lesson. Do this twice with different content.

4
Read both versions aloud — let your partner call it

Read your real tricolon, then your anti-tricolon. Your partner says which one lands harder. If the anti-tricolon somehow sounds better, your "item three" isn't actually the heaviest — go back and find the right one.

The breath test

Are you slightly out of breath at the end of item three? If the sentence ends too easily, it hasn't earned the landing. Expand item three until it takes real effort to finish.

Sketchnote It

Draw a staircase in your notebook — three steps, each one higher than the last. Label each step with an item from your tricolon. Show item three as the tallest step, with the most text or the biggest visual weight. Add an arrow showing the direction of build. Bonus: draw the anti-tricolon as a staircase going the wrong way — tallest step first, then descending.

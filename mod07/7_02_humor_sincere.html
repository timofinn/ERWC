7.02 | Humor → Sincere Thursday, May 8

Chase Dahl

Weber High School, Class of 2015 — Pleasant View, Utah

The Lens: Humor → Wholesome
What this lens does
Ahhh the classic "Wedding speech" format: Win them with laughs, awe them with heart. Think of the joke as the knock on the door — pull off your punchline, they let you in. Continually telling jokes without ever turning the corner to something more substantive, and you risk being perceived as strictly facetious. Eventually you have to make that shift to a heartfelt message that brings a tear to gam gam's eyes.

Humor Is Harder Than It Looks

Writing a joke that lands is actually pretty tough. You're playing to a room that ranges from third graders to grandparents, so that inside joke that kills with your friends... yeah, good luck. So the question remains: how can I be funny for an audience that large?

The speakers who pull this off aren't necessarily funnier — they just shoot for safer targets. They go after the stuff everyone recognizes: the awkwardness of being seventeen, the absurdity of the bells, the weirdness of this specific moment. They aren't roasting people. They're just inviting everyone in on the joke.

What to Watch For

  • Where does the humor actually land — and who is it aimed at? Does it include the whole room or just the graduating class?
  • Find the pivot. There's a specific moment where the tone shifts. What signals it — a word, a sentence, a pause?
  • What does Chase actually argue by the end? Strip away every joke. What's the real claim?
  • Notice how long he stays in humor mode before he pivots. That ratio is a choice — and it affects whether the sincerity feels earned or sudden.

The Move in Action

The Humor — Early in the speech
"We danced to lyrics we did not understand in the song 'Gangnam Style.' We said 'YOLO' and 'cray-cray' as if we were supposed to. Thank goodness we have 'yeet' now."
What he's doing here

He's not doing stand-up — he's doing inventory. Listing the cultural garbage you all survived together. The joke isn't really a joke, it's a collective cringe. Everyone in that room has a Gangnam Style memory, even the grandparents who sat through it confused. That shared recognition is what makes it land — it belongs to everyone, not just his friends.

The Pivot — Where the tone turns
"Now, at the end of our careers as students, we have an opportunity. Now is our time to take on the world, to find and pursue our passions — to 'leave a legacy.'"
What he's doing here

Watch how he lands the plane. He doesn't announce the shift — he just makes it. The humor stops, the pace slows, and the word "opportunity" signals to the audience: okay, we're in a different part of the speech now. The fact that he put "leave a legacy" in quotes is interesting too — he's aware it's a cliché, which gives him a little ironic distance even as he's being sincere. That's a smart move.

The Landing — What he's actually arguing
"To 'leave a legacy' and to 'achieve greatness' is not to gain money and recognition. It is to leave those you cross paths with a little more happiness and hope."
What he's doing here

This is the thesis — and it arrives about two-thirds of the way through a speech that spent most of its time making you laugh. He's redefining what success means at a moment when everyone in that room is about to walk out and try to figure that out. It's a simple argument, but the humor earned him the right to make it without it sounding like a bumper sticker.

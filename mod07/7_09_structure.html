Chiasmus · Hypophora · Parallelism

All or nothing.
Chiasmus A-B becomes B-A — the flip reverses the logic.
Why it works: It mirrors the structure to snap a truth into place. The "flip" makes a simple point sound like a revelation because the structure makes it feel deep, even when it isn't.
See Some Examples
"Speak in such a way that others love to listen to you. Listen in such a way that others love to speak to you."
— Unknown
"It's not the men in my life that counts, it's the life in my men."
— Mae West
"People don't have ideas; ideas have people."
— Carl Jung
"They must find it difficult… those who have taken authority as the truth, rather than truth as the authority."
— Gerald Massey
"It's not the size of the dog in the fight; it's the size of the fight in the dog."
— Mark Twain
"We don't stop playing because we grow old; we grow old because we stop playing."
— George Bernard Shaw
"I am a marvelous housekeeper. Every time I leave a man, I keep his house."
— Zsa Zsa Gabor
"15–20 years ago, the internet was an escape from the real world. Now, the real world is an escape from the internet."
— Noah Smith, Noahpinion (paraphrased)
Weak vs. Strong
Weak
"You make the school great, and the school makes you great."
Strong
"We didn't shape this place. This place shaped us — and now it's time to find out what we do with the shape we're in."
Why?
The original just says two nice things that don't flip anything. The revision reverses the direction of influence — and extends the metaphor into something new.
Weak
"Work hard so you can play hard."
Strong
"We didn't come here to learn how to work. We came here to figure out what's worth working for."
Why?
"Work hard, play hard" is a sequence, not a flip. The revision reverses what learning is actually about — work toward purpose, not reward.
Skip to the Assignment
1
Find a real reversal — not a restatement

Chiasmus only works when the flip actually inverts the logic. "We made the school great and the school made us great" is not a chiasmus — both halves say the same thing. You need a genuine A→B then B→A where the direction of the relationship changes. Write down what relationship or idea you want to flip before you write a single word.

2
Use the scaffold: "Not A, but B / Not B, but A"

This is the fastest way to find the shape. Write "Not ___, but ___" for the first half. Then flip it: "Not ___, but ___" in reverse. Once you have the shape, you can drop the "Not" and tighten the language. Write three attempts. Cross out the two that don't genuinely reverse the logic. Keep the one where the second half turns the first inside out.

3
Tighten it — then extend it

A chiasmus should be short enough to land in one breath. Trim every word that isn't doing work. Then, if you want, extend the thought after the flip — like the Watts example: "You play the piano. You don't work the piano." The flip is complete in six words. Everything after it is the argument that follows from the flip. Two drafts, two different reversals.

4
Read it aloud — wait for the double-take

A good chiasmus makes the listener pause. They hear it, process the flip, and then nod. Read it to your partner and watch their face. No reaction = the flip didn't land. A pause and a nod = it worked. If they say "wait, say that again" — that's the best possible sign.

The test

Your partner repeats the chiasmus back to you from memory. If they can't, it's either too complicated or the flip wasn't memorable enough. Simplify until it sticks in one hearing.

Sketchnote It

Draw an X — that's the shape of chiasmus. Label the top-left with A, the top-right with B. Then at the bottom, show B on the left and A on the right, flipped. Write your two key words in the four corners. In the center where the lines cross, write the word or idea that the flip reveals. The X is the device. The center is the argument.

Hypophora Ask the question. Answer it yourself. Control the room.
Why it works: You voice the unspoken question in the audience's head, then answer it immediately. It seizes control of their internal monologue and answers the question for them before they have time to decide.
See Some Examples
"Between two evils, I always pick the one I never tried before. Why? Because I'm curious."
— Mae West
"What did high school teach me? Not the quadratic formula. Not the periodic table. It taught me that the person sitting next to you on the worst day of your life — that's the education."
— Original example
Weak vs. Strong
Weak
"What is the meaning of life? It's to be happy and help others."
Strong
"What did four years of high school actually teach us? Not the quadratic formula. Not the periodic table. It taught us that the person sitting next to you on the worst day of your life — that's the education."
Why?
"Meaning of life" is too abstract — the audience has heard it a thousand times. "What did high school actually teach us" is immediate and in the room. The unexpected answer reframes the entire four years.
Weak
"Why are we here today? We're here to celebrate graduation."
Strong
"Why does this moment feel so strange? Because for the first time in twelve years, nobody is telling us where to sit, when to speak, or how long we have. That feeling isn't confusion. That's freedom — and it's terrifying."
Why?
"Why are we here" has an obvious answer that kills the momentum. "Why does this feel strange" names what everyone is actually feeling. The answer validates the audience's experience — which is the most powerful thing a speaker can do.
Skip to the Assignment
1
Find the question already in the room

The question in a hypophora isn't a question you invented — it's one the audience is already silently asking. Write down three things your audience is probably thinking right now: about this moment, about what comes next, about high school, about themselves. Those are your candidate questions. The best hypophora voices what people are already feeling.

2
Kill the obvious question — find the unexpected answer

"Why are we here?" has an obvious answer that kills momentum. "Why does this moment feel so strange?" names what people are actually experiencing. Cross out the question with an obvious answer. Keep the one that needs a real answer. Then write the answer — and make it land somewhere unexpected. The answer is the argument. Don't waste it on what they already know.

3
Draft: question, then answer across 2–4 sentences

Ask the question. Then answer it — not in one word, but in 2–4 sentences that build toward a specific, surprising conclusion. The answer earns the question. Write two drafts, each with a different question drawn from your candidate list.

4
Read it aloud — check if the question felt real

Read it to your partner. One question for them: did the opening question feel like it came from the room, or did it feel like a setup? A setup = rewrite the question. If the question felt real, ask them: did the answer surprise you, or did you already know it was coming? Already knew it = find a less obvious answer.

The test

Two things your partner checks: (1) Did the question feel like something they were actually wondering? (2) Did the answer land somewhere unexpected? Both need to be yes.

Sketchnote It

Draw a thought bubble on the left — that's the audience's unspoken question. Draw a speech bubble on the right — that's your answer. Connect them with an arrow pointing from the thought bubble to the speech bubble: you're intercepting their internal monologue and redirecting it. Inside the thought bubble, write the question in keywords. Inside the speech bubble, write the core of your unexpected answer. Surround the answer bubble with small icons that capture what makes it surprising.

Parallelism Same grammatical structure, repeated — ideas land as one.
Why it works: It binds separate ideas into a single, rhythmic strike. By matching the structure, you force the audience to swallow the whole argument in one breath.
See Some Examples
"With malice toward none, with charity for all, with firmness in the right."
— Abraham Lincoln, Second Inaugural Address, 1865
"We shall pay any price, bear any burden, meet any hardship, support any friend, oppose any foe."
— John F. Kennedy, Inaugural Address, 1961
"We stayed up late finishing it. We woke up early defending it. We showed up anyway, every single time."
— Original example
Weak vs. Strong
Weak
"She worked hard, stayed focused, and there was never a moment she gave up."
Strong
"She worked hard, she stayed focused, and she never gave up."
Why?
"There was never a moment she gave up" breaks the verb pattern — it's a clause, not a phrase. Keeping all three as simple verb phrases locks the rhythm. Three beats, equal weight, clean landing.
Weak
"We came to learn, we came to grow, and making friends was also something we did."
Strong
"We came to learn. We came to grow. We came to find the people who would make both of those things feel worth it."
Why?
The third item in the original breaks the "we came to ___" pattern. The fix keeps the structure and applies the tricolon rule: the third item carries the most weight.
Skip to the Assignment
1
Choose your grammatical template

Parallelism starts with a repeating grammatical shape. Pick one before you write any content. Options: "We came to ___." / "She ___, she ___, she ___." / "With ___, with ___, with ___." / "[Verb] any ___." Write your template at the top of the page and commit to it. Every item has to fit the same mold.

2
Draft three items — then check the grammar, not just the meaning

Write your three items using the template. Then read them back and check: is each item grammatically identical in structure? A common mistake is letting the third item become a clause when the first two were phrases. "She worked hard, she stayed focused, and there was never a moment she gave up" — the third one breaks the mold. Fix it: "She worked hard, she stayed focused, she never gave up."

3
Apply the tricolon rule to item three

Parallelism and tricolon work together. Keep all three items grammatically parallel — but make item three carry the most weight in content. Same shape, more meaning. Write two drafts using two different grammatical templates.

4
Read it aloud — listen for the rhythm breaking

Read it to your partner. They close their eyes and listen. If one item sounds different in length or shape, they raise their hand at that item. Wherever they raise their hand — that's the broken link. Match it to the template and try again.

The test

Your partner taps the desk once for each item as you read. The taps should feel evenly spaced — same rhythm, same shape. If one tap comes early or late, a link broke. Find it.

Sketchnote It

Draw three identical containers — boxes, circles, whatever — arranged in a row or a column. Each container holds one item from your parallel series. The containers should be the same shape and size to show the matching structure. Inside each one, write the key word or phrase. Use arrows or lines to show they're part of the same chain. If you're applying the tricolon rule, shade or bold the third container to show it carries more weight — same shape, more inside.

Master Class See them woven together in one excerpt.

One passage. Multiple structural devices working simultaneously. Alan Watts was a philosopher and lecturer — not a speechwriter — but this excerpt is a clinic in how chiasmus, hypophora, and parallelism do the work of argument. Read it once straight through. Then open the breakdowns.

"The existence, the physical universe is basically playful. There is no necessity for it whatsoever. It isn't going anywhere. That is to say, it doesn't have some destination that it ought to arrive at. But it is best understood by analogy with music, because music, as an art form is essentially playful. We say, 'You play the piano.' You don't work the piano. Why? Music differs from, say, travel. When you travel, you are trying to get somewhere. In music, though, one doesn't make the end of the composition the point of the composition. If that were so, the best conductors would be those who played fastest. And there would be composers who only wrote finales. People would go to a concert just to hear one crackling chord… because that's the end! Same way with dancing. You don't aim at a particular spot in the room because that's where you will arrive. The whole point of the dancing is the dance. But we don't see that as something brought by our education into our conduct... It's coming, it's coming, that great thing. The success you're working for. Then you wake up one day about 40 years old and you say, 'My God, I've arrived. I'm there.' And you don't feel very different from what you've always felt... It was a musical thing, and you were supposed to sing or to dance while the music was being played."
— Alan Watts, "Life and Music"
Chiasmus — "Play the piano / Work the piano"

Watts doesn't announce his argument — he flips it into place in six words. This is chiasmus at its most efficient: the structural reversal carries the entire philosophical distinction between play and labor.

"You play the piano. You don't work the piano."

A = play, B = piano. The flip reverses the verb and reassigns the meaning of the whole activity. He doesn't explain the difference between play and work. He makes you feel it in the structure of the sentence.

Later, the same logic governs the closing line: life was supposed to be played, not worked toward. The chiasmus at the opening is the seed of the argument. The ending is where it blooms.

Hypophora — "Why?"

One word. That's the whole question. Watts drops it mid-thought and then answers it himself across the next several sentences — seizing control of the audience's internal monologue at the exact moment they might start forming their own objection.

"We say, 'You play the piano.' You don't work the piano. Why? Music differs from, say, travel. When you travel, you are trying to get somewhere. In music, though, one doesn't make the end of the composition the point of the composition."

Notice what he does: he asks the question before the audience can ask it themselves. That's the move. If he'd just kept talking, listeners might have mentally pushed back. By voicing the objection first, he disarms it — and then answers it on his own terms.

Parallelism — The Treadmill Sequence

The central section of the passage is built on relentless parallel structure — each grade, each milestone, each "then" stacking on the last. The rhythm mimics the very treadmill he's critiquing. By the time "it's coming, it's coming" arrives, you feel the anxiety in the structure itself.

"You go to kindergarten and that's a great thing because when you finish that you get into first grade. First grade leads to second grade and so on. Then you get out of grade school and you got high school... then you're going to go to college… then you've got graduate school... And all the time that thing is coming — it's coming, it's coming."

Each clause has the same grammatical shape: milestone → next milestone. The structure keeps moving forward and never arrives — which is exactly his point. The form enacts the argument. You don't just hear the treadmill. You feel it.

The Architecture of the Whole Passage

Watts structures this passage the way a musician structures a composition — theme, variation, climax, resolution. Notice how each rhetorical move hands off to the next:

Introduce the theme — chiasmus: play vs. work, set in six words.
Voice the objection — hypophora: "Why?" answered before you can ask it.
Build the pressure — parallelism: the treadmill, grade by grade.
The crash — "My God, I've arrived. I'm there." And nothing feels different.
Resolution — return to the opening image: you were supposed to dance.

The closing line isn't a new idea. It's the opening idea, returned. That's what good rhetoric does — it doesn't conclude, it resolves. Like music.

Watts isn't reaching for devices. He's thinking hard about something that matters — and the devices follow. That's the goal for your speech. Find the thing worth saying. The tools will do the rest.

Device Reference

The Nine Devices — Quick Lookup

You've studied these. Use this as a fast reminder while you draft. The goal isn't to force all nine in — it's to find the five (or more) that already fit what you're saying, and make them land harder.

Device What it does Speech example
Anaphora Repeats a word or phrase at the start of consecutive clauses "We stayed late. We came early. We showed up."
Antistrophe Repeats at the end of clauses "This class built something. This class lost something. This class became something."
Tricolon Three parallel items, building in force "We were loud, we were tired, we were unstoppable."
Antithesis Puts opposites side by side to create contrast "The year started in chaos. It ended in clarity."
Metaphor Calls one thing another to fuse their meanings "Senior year was a construction site — nothing looked finished until suddenly it was."
Analogy Draws a comparison to make a complex idea clear "Graduating is like finishing a long hike — you don't feel proud until you look back."
Chiasmus Reverses the structure of two parallel phrases "We didn't just leave this school — this school left something in us."
Hypophora Asks a question, then immediately answers it "Did we know this would be hard? We did. Did we do it anyway? Look around."
Parallelism Repeats a grammatical structure across phrases "To the freshmen: stay curious. To the juniors: hold on. To my class: we made it."
Label Your Devices

Minimum Five. At Least Two Types.

Don't just drop a device in — make sure it's earning its keep. A device that fits sounds natural. One that's forced sticks out. Find the spots in your draft where the language already has rhythm or contrast, then identify what's happening.

How to label in Google Docs: Highlight the phrase → Insert → Comment → Type the device name + one sentence on what it does.

Before You Submit

Self-Check

Run through this before you hand it in. If you can't check a box, fix it first.

  • The hook is specific — not "high school was a journey" but a scene, a question, or a line that earns its spot.
  • The body has at least one story that's anchored in time and place.
  • There's a turn where "I" becomes "we."
  • The closing echoes or answers something from the opening.
  • I labeled at least five rhetorical devices with comments in the doc.
  • The draft would take 4–6 minutes to read aloud at a normal pace. (If you're not sure — read it aloud right now and time it.)
  • Nothing on the Mistakes list from 7.12 made it into the draft.
